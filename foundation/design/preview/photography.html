Brand · Photography

Real people, real moments — warm but never staged.

Photography is where the foundation's voice becomes a face. Pictures should feel like you stumbled into a moment, not a marketing shoot. Light, candor and connection over polish.

01

Photo categories

Five archetypes do most of the work. Knowing which one a moment is helps with cropping, captioning and where it lives on the page.

Community

The room, full of people who showed up.

Wide-angle group photos at events. Use to convey scale and warmth. Crop loose — the chaos of a real group is the point.

Best at: 16:9 heroUsed on: Concert page, recap email
Founders

Wendy & Rob, the people behind it.

Candid two-shots of the founders. Eye contact, warm light, never on a step-and-repeat. Use sparingly — when copy needs a face.

Best at: 1:1 or 4:5Used on: About, founder letters
Place

Geist Reservoir at golden hour.

The lake is part of the brand. Wide horizons, water + sky, never with people in frame. Use for atmospheric breaks and section openers.

Best at: 16:9, full bleedUsed on: Hero, between sections
In memory

The people we're chasing the cure for.

Reference photographs of loved ones — Sue Chase and the families behind the foundation. Treat with reverence. Always with a name and a date.

Best at: 4:5 portraitUsed on: Story, dedication panels
Partner

With MJFF, the science partner.

Photos at MJFF events show legitimacy and connection. These can be a bit more posed — they're proof, not a moment. Always credit the event.

Best at: 4:5 or 1:1Used on: Science page, partner blocks
Object

The raffle boat, the auction items.

Product-style photography for tangible items: raffle boats, auction packages. Clean backgrounds, isolated subject. Less art, more honesty.

Best at: 16:9 wideUsed on: Concert raffle, sponsors
02

Cropping

Same photo, three crops. The hero crop should breathe; the square is for cards; the portrait is for sidebars and stacked email layouts.

16:9 — HeroHeadlines sit on bottom-left. Sky on top, water on bottom.
1:1 — CardTighter on the subject. Use in grids, social, donor walls.
4:5 — PortraitEmail sidebars, mobile hero, IG feed.
03

Treatments

Default to true color. Reserve the warm grade for hero moments where mood matters. Duotone only for archival, in-memory or section-divider use — never for live event coverage.

True color · default

How real people remember the moment. Use 90% of the time.

Warm grade · feature

+10% saturation, +5% contrast. For hero photos and recap covers only.

Duotone · archival

Orange × ink-900 over grayscale. Used for dedications, in-memory, history.

04

Type over photo

Always darken the bottom of a hero with a 0→65% black gradient. Headline lives in the bottom-left; eyebrow in orange-300; never set type on a busy mid-frame area.

05

Do & don't

A few hard rules. The first three matter most.

Do — candid eye contact, soft light

Founders feel like people you'd talk to. Imperfect framing is fine.

Don't — step-and-repeat as primary hero

Acceptable for partner/proof blocks. Never the front-page emotional anchor.

Do — let the lake breathe

Wide horizons, sky weight, no people in frame for atmospheric breaks.

Don't — corporate headshots as story art

Headshots are for board pages. Don't open a story or campaign with one.

The eight principles, on one screen.

  1. Real over stagedCandid beats posed. We are not a corporate brand.
  2. Warm light, true colorGolden hour and natural daylight. No heavy filters.
  3. Faces, not crowds-from-aboveEye level. People should feel approachable.
  4. Place is part of the storyThe lake, the boats, the Indiana sky — they belong.
  5. Reverence for memory photosAlways named, always dated, never cropped tight.
  6. Darken before you set type0→65% gradient bottom. Never type on busy mid-frame.
  7. Crop with intent16:9 to breathe, 1:1 to focus, 4:5 to elevate.
  8. One photo per momentPick the best frame. Galleries dilute. Resist the carousel.
