Brand · Voice & tone

Direct, science-forward, and built from love.

Most nonprofit copy reaches for emotion and oversells. We do the opposite — we tell the truth about the science, name what the money does, and let the reader decide. Warmth comes from candor, not adjectives.

01

Voice — what we sound like, always

Voice doesn't change. Tone shifts with context. These four attributes describe the voice — every piece of copy, from a tweet to a tax letter, should sound like all four at once.

Direct, not breathless.

Plain sentences. Active verbs. The reader's time matters. We don't bury the ask, the impact, or the math.

We are
Plain-spoken. One idea per sentence.
Not
Urgent. Capital-letter-y. Padded with adjectives.

Science-forward, not preachy.

The work is real research at MJFF. We name it, point to it, and trust the reader to follow. We don't lecture or moralize.

We are
Specific. Cite trials, papers, dollar figures.
Not
"Together we will make a difference." Vague hope.

Personal, not corporate.

The foundation came from a family. The voice is a person — usually Wendy — talking to another person, not a brand to a market.

We are
"I" and "we." First names. Lake-house casual.
Not
"Our organization is committed to…"

Hopeful, not desperate.

The science is closer than people realize. We say so. We don't manufacture crisis or guilt to move dollars — that voice doesn't belong to us.

We are
Confident. The work is moving. Join in.
Not
"Time is running out." "We can't do this without you."

03

Write this, not that

Six concrete pairs. Read the bad version aloud — that's what most charity copy sounds like. The good version is what we sound like.

✓ Write this Donation page hero

"100% of your gift reaches MJFF research. No salaries, no overhead — we cover those ourselves."

Why: A clean fact, said plainly. Trust comes from specificity.

✕ Not this Generic charity copy

"Your generous support empowers us to make a meaningful impact in the fight against this devastating disease."

Why not: No specifics. Five adjectives. Sounds like every other ask.

✓ Write this Email subject

"What your $250 funded last quarter."

Why: Concrete, owed to the donor, no exclamation point.

✕ Not this Email subject

"You won't believe the difference YOU made!! 💜"

Why not: Begs for the click. Caps and emoji do not equal warmth.

✓ Write this About page intro

"Wendy lost her dad to Parkinson's. So she started a concert. Eight years and $487,000 later, it's still going."

Why: Story, then number. The math earns the feeling.

✕ Not this About page intro

"Our journey began with a passion to fight Parkinson's, and it has grown into something truly special."

Why not: "Journey," "passion," "truly special" — the holy trinity of nonprofit slop.

✓ Write this Concert page CTA

"Tickets are $150. The boat raffle is $100 for one entry, $250 for three. Doors at 5."

Why: Pricing first. The reader is here to commit, not to be inspired.

✕ Not this Concert page CTA

"Join us for an unforgettable evening of music, community, and hope as we come together for a cause that matters."

Why not: Says nothing actionable. Reader still doesn't know the price.

✓ Write this In-memory dedication

"For Sue Chase. 1947–2021. The reason."

Why: Reverent. Three lines. Doesn't try to write a eulogy.

✕ Not this In-memory dedication

"In loving memory of those who have battled this terrible disease and inspired us all to keep fighting."

Why not: No name. "Battled," "terrible disease" — clichés the family wouldn't use.

✓ Write this Social — concert recap

"312 of you. $67,200 to MJFF. Same lake, same song, one year closer."

Why: Numbers do the work. The last clause is the only flourish, and it's earned.

✕ Not this Social — concert recap

"WOW! What a night! 🎶✨ So grateful for our amazing community! Together, we're changing lives! 💪"

Why not: Five exclamations, no facts. Could be any nonprofit, any night.

04

Voice on each surface

Same voice, different rhythm. Headlines lean lyrical; receipts lean clean; receipts also keep one warm sentence so they don't read like a bank email.

Hero headline

Closer than people realize.

Italic serif, one short clause, one orange word. Don't explain it underneath — let it carry weight.

Length: 3–6 wordsAvoid: Two clauses

Donation receipt

Thank you, Maria.

Your gift is on its way to the science. Every dollar reaches MJFF — no salaries, no skim.

Then: the receipt details, plain and clean.

Length: 2 short paragraphsVoice: Personal + plain

Founder letter

"We started this because Parkinson's took someone we love."

First-person, full sentences, the way Wendy actually talks. Italic serif when quoted; sans when in body.

Length: 3–5 paragraphsVoice: Personal + confident

Social — call to action

$25 = half a day of research.

Concrete impact, equal sign, period. Pair with one photo. Don't add hashtags about hope.

Length: Under 10 wordsVoice: Direct
05

Words we use & avoid

Vocabulary signals voice faster than sentence structure. Use the left column. Avoid the right.

USE

  • the scienceSpecific, ownable. Beats "research" alone.
  • biomarkerThe actual work MJFF is doing.
  • fund / fundedActive, financial, specific.
  • reaches MJFFNames the partner. Builds trust.
  • built from loveTagline. Use sparingly.
  • closerHopeful without hype.
  • concertAlways lowercase, never "the Concert event."

AVOID

  • journeyHas no meaning left. Replace with what actually happened.
  • fight / battleDon't militarize a chronic illness.
  • warriorsPatients are not soldiers.
  • devastatingTells the reader how to feel.
  • impactIf you must, say what changed.
  • amazing / incredibleEmpty intensifiers.
  • communityAcceptable rarely. "People who showed up" is better.
