01 · The Narrative

The story, in her own words.

Read this out loud before you write anything in Wendy's voice. It's the full arc — origin, conviction, ask — told the way she would tell it to someone sitting across from her at a dinner table. Every sentence here can be lifted, shortened, or rearranged into a letter, an email, a speech, or a social post.

On Christmas morning, 2017, I sat at the breakfast table and faced a truth no daughter wants to face: I could not save my mom.

Sue had extraordinary doctors. A deeply trusted care team. Every treatment Rob and I knew how to pursue — and between us, nearly sixty years in pharma, biotech, and health tech, we knew how to pursue a lot. We prayed, and we never stopped praying. We fought for every possible option. It still was not enough.

It was a different kind of Christmas. I could not think of a single gift that might bring my mother hope. So I made a different kind of decision: we would raise money to help other families. That's what I could give her.

Four months later, in April 2018, we founded Chasing a Cure Parkinson's Foundation. Sue was still alive. We told her what we were doing. She died that December. What began as heartbreak became a promise — to keep going, for every other family facing the same fear, the same pain, the same uncertainty we had faced.

So here is what this foundation is. No one here takes a salary — not Rob, not me, not a single board member. We keep operations as lean as a foundation legally can: the costs to run events, process your donation, and keep the lights on. Everything beyond that flows directly to The Michael J. Fox Foundation and Team Fox, which is the most rigorous, most respected Parkinson's research engine in the world. Parkinson's has touched both of our families. My godfather. Rob's aunt. This is not abstract to us, and we don't talk about it like it is.

I don't want your pity. I want your partnership. Ten million people are living with this disease right now. One million of them are in this country. Ninety thousand more will be diagnosed this year — one every six minutes — and not one of them has a cure to go home to.

But the science is moving. That is the part I want you to hear. The science is moving, and it is close enough that funding it now, funding it today, funding it this year, actually changes when we get there. That's not a marketing line. That's what twenty-plus years in this industry have taught me.

So here is what I'm asking. Give if you can. Come to the concert. Buy a raffle ticket. Send this to one person. Whatever you do, do it because you believe — like I do — that the cure is close enough to fund now.

Science fuels hope. We fund the science behind breakthroughs. That's the whole thing.

Wendy Wendy Chase · Co-founder
02 · Her Character

Who Wendy is on the page.

If you're writing as Wendy, or quoting her, or writing about the foundation in a way that needs to feel like her, these are the traits to keep in your head. She is a specific person, not a template.

She is a scientist first, and a daughter second — but never one without the other.

Wendy does not talk about Parkinson's in abstractions. She knows the biology. She knows the funding pipeline. She knows what a Phase II trial costs and why Team Fox is the right partner. That expertise gives her standing. But she never leads with it — she leads with Sue, with her godfather, with Rob's aunt. The science is the evidence. The family is the reason.

She is direct. She does not perform grief.

Wendy will tell you her mother died. She will not dwell on it. She will not ask you to feel bad. She will tell you what she did next, and what she's asking you to do now. The grief is in the room — she doesn't have to decorate it.

She is warm without being soft.

She says family a lot. She uses first names. She means them. But underneath the warmth there is a spine of conviction that doesn't bend: the cure is close, we are funding it, your gift flows directly to the work. She is polite, but she's not here to be charming.

She is hopeful on purpose.

Hope, for Wendy, is not a mood. It's a decision. She chooses it because the alternative — despair, cynicism, performance — doesn't fund any science. When she says the science is moving, she means it literally, and she expects you to take her seriously.

"I don't want your pity. I want your partnership."
03 · Cadence

How she talks — rhythm & shape.

Wendy's sentences have a recognizable shape. Match the shape and the voice follows.

Short sentences carry the weight.

When something matters, Wendy says it in five words or fewer. Sue died that December. There is no cure. Not yet. That's the whole thing. The short sentences are never dramatic — they're just the truth, put plainly.

Long sentences build the argument.

When she explains, she uses flowing sentences with clear commas — she'll list three things, she'll stack a claim on top of a qualifier, she'll pull you through the reasoning and then land on something short. Because the science is moving, and it is close enough that funding it today actually changes when we get there — that's not a marketing line, that's what my career has taught me.

She uses the rule of three.

Built from love. Backed by science. Focused on the cure. Three is her number. Watch for it in her writing: three pillars, three promises, three facts, three asks. When you need to make something feel settled, use three.

She repeats herself on purpose.

Fund the science. Fund the hope. Fund the work. Repetition is how she lands the point. Don't apologize for it. Don't "vary your language." When a phrase is working, say it again.

She ends where she began.

Almost every long-form thing she writes closes with a callback to the opening — usually to Sue, or to the promise. That loop is what makes the piece feel complete instead of ended.

05 · Guardrails

What she doesn't say.

Wendy's voice is defined as much by what she refuses as by what she says. If any of these appear in copy under her name, something has gone wrong.

She would say

  • Live with Parkinson's. A state of being, not a verdict.
  • Family. Hers, yours, the ones waiting.
  • Fund the science. Move it forward. Advance it.
  • The cure. Not "a cure" — the cure. Specific. Real.
  • Not yet. Two words that hold every other hopeful thing she says.
  • Breakthrough. Because that's what the money buys.
  • Sue. Her mother has a name. Use it.
  • Volunteer-run. No salaries. True, specific, defensible.
  • Directly to research through MJFF and Team Fox. Names the pathway.

She would not say

  • Fight. We're not fighting Parkinson's — we're funding the end of it.
  • Suffer / sufferer / victim. Reductive and inaccurate.
  • Battle, warrior, brave journey. Disease-charity boilerplate.
  • Awareness as a goal. Awareness is a step toward funding. Funding is the goal.
  • Give back. Too generic. Say what the money does.
  • Help us help them. She doesn't position donors as saviors.
  • 100% of every dollar / every penny / zero overhead. We do have operating costs (events, processing fees, tools). We never claim otherwise. Say "directly to research," "no salaries," or "as lean as a foundation legally can."
  • Exclamation points. She doesn't need them. Neither do you.
06 · In conversation

How she'd answer.

Real prompts she gets asked. The answers below are written in her voice and can be lifted as quotes, used in interviews, or adapted for talking points.

Why did you start the foundation?
Because I sat at the breakfast table on Christmas morning, 2017, and I faced a truth no daughter wants to face: I could not save my mom. Sue had extraordinary doctors, every treatment Rob and I knew how to pursue, and between us we've spent our whole careers in this industry. It still was not enough. I could not think of a gift that might bring her hope — so I made a different kind of decision. We would raise money to help other families. Chasing a Cure is what grew out of that morning.
What makes you different from other Parkinson's charities?
Three things. No one here draws a salary — not Rob, not me, not a single board member — so our costs are the bare minimum a foundation can legally run on. We route the funds through The Michael J. Fox Foundation, so the research is world-class. And we're founder-led by two people with sixty years of pharma experience between them. You're not donating into a bureaucracy. You're handing a dollar to someone who knows exactly where it's going.
How close is a cure, really?
Closer than most people think, and further than any of us would like. The science is moving. There are therapies in trials right now that would have been science fiction when my mother was diagnosed. Whether they land in five years or fifteen depends, in part, on whether we keep funding them. That's the honest answer.
What would you say to someone whose parent was just diagnosed?
I would say: I know. I know what this week feels like. I would tell them to find the best care team they can, to ask every question, and to pursue every treatment they can pursue — because Sue had all of that, and it mattered, even when it wasn't enough. And I would tell them the science is moving. You don't have to start a foundation. You just have to not let the fear be the last word.
Why should I give $100 to you instead of a bigger charity?
You shouldn't give instead of a bigger charity — you should give to one, through us. We exist because we route money, directly, into the best Parkinson's research organization in the world. No one here takes a salary. We keep our operating costs to the bare minimum. Your $100 reaches MJFF and Team Fox with almost nothing taken off the top — and because we're small and local, we can tell you exactly where it went.
07 · By channel

Her voice, tuned for the room.

Same voice everywhere, different volume. Think of these as dials, not different voices.

In a personal email or letter

She writes like she's talking to one person. Hi Jennifer. She opens with something real — a detail about them, or a moment from her week. She says what she's writing about early, usually in the first three sentences. She closes with gratitude and a first name. She does not sign as "Wendy Chase, Co-founder." Just Wendy.

On stage or in a video

She slows down. She uses Sue's name at least once. She pauses after the short sentences. She ends with one of the three anchor lines — usually Built from love. Backed by science. Focused on the cure. — because it's designed to be said out loud.

On social media

One idea per post. No thread-bait. No hashtag soup. She might quote one of her own lines, or one number, or one sentence from this document. She does not chase trends. She does not use memes. She does not explain the urgency — she names it.

In a press quote

She speaks in full sentences that stand alone. She always has one number and one name ready: Ninety thousand new diagnoses a year. My mother was one of them. Reporters take that quote every time.

In a sponsorship conversation

She's professional, specific, and quick to the point. She doesn't pitch emotion — she pitches alignment. She names the sponsor's audience, the foundation's audience, and the overlap. Then she asks for a specific thing: a check amount, a table, a logo placement, a match. She is comfortable being direct because she is asking for the research, not for herself.
