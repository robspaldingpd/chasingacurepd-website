### Placement decisions — the answers to your explicit questions

- Check-in: just inside the front door on the main floor. One small table, two chairs (for the Check-In volunteer + Rob or Wendy for the first 20 minutes). Name tags + paddle numbers + We Connect card + QR card in a pre-labeled envelope.
- Welcome pour: one step past check-in, on the main floor. A dedicated server (caterer's most poised person) with a tray of Veuve and one elevated NA option. Scripted line: 'A pour of Sue's favorite — Veuve — welcome.'
- Butterfly wall: main floor, on the natural path between welcome pour and the kitchen/dining area. Staged as a Sallie-designed installation (see Section 10). Every guest walks past it in the first two minutes without being asked to.
- Food: main floor. Chef action at the kitchen island (visible); passed hors d'oeuvres circulate from the kitchen; grazing table in the dining area.
- Bar: basement. Guests naturally drift down for the full experience; the deck-level crowd sends a runner (or Rob / a caterer) up with a beer or wine tray during Act I so the basement doesn't become a separate event.
- Formal speaking + fundraising: backyard + pool. One focal point. Everyone is gathered there for the 4:00–5:05 program block.
- Basement during program: doors stay open, bar stays staffed, but the full bar 'goes quiet' — no shaker noise during Kiera or the toast. Bartender has a laminated cue sheet with the moment-by-moment volume signal. After program: full service resumes at 5:05.
### How to keep the event from feeling scattered

- Act I is designed to be dispersed — that is the point. Scattered is a feature during cocktail hour. A good elevated cocktail party uses every room.
- Act II pulls everyone together. The band's call-in song + the Floor Captain's gather sweep + the audio system dropping all zones to silence = one unmistakable cue that it's time.
- Act III releases intentionally. Band kicks off the peak set ON the deck — which means the deck and backyard are one sonic environment. Dessert opens on the main floor — which gives the dance-averse a destination. Bar fully back on in the basement.

### Why this works without a screen

- The sound system IS the reveal. When a live phone call comes through the backyard speakers and 100 people hear a stranger learn they just won a boat, there is nothing a screen could add.
- Dan is the visual. His presence, his voice, his story — that is the production value. A screen would compete with him.
- The iPad + Rafflebox handles the 'is this real' trust question. Wendy reads the winner's name straight from the dashboard; Rob shows the iPad to the front row. Nobody doubts the draw.

### How the tracker is organized

- Tab 1 — Weekly Tracker. Every task for every week from T-13 to event day + follow-up. Columns: week / target date, workstream, task, owner, support person, deadline, dependency, status, founder review needed, notes.
- Tab 2 — Founder-Only Decisions. Decisions that must stay with Wendy (and where noted, Rob).

### How to run the tracker (30 minutes per Monday)

- Open the Weekly Tracker tab. Filter to the current week. Update every status: Not started / In progress / Done / Blocked.
- Everything Blocked — send one email or text that clears the blocker today.
- Every 'Founder review needed = YES' row — build the answer in the next 48 hours.
- Read the next week's tasks to stay ahead of anything that slips.
- Close: one sentence in the Notes column on the biggest win of the week and the biggest risk.
### Status color convention inside the tracker

- Green — Done.
- Amber — In progress, on track.
- Red — Blocked or slipping.
- Grey — Not started (OK if not yet its week).
