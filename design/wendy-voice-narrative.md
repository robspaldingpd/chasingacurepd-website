## 01 · The Narrative

### The story, in her own words.

Read this out loud before you write anything in Wendy's voice. It's the full arc — origin, conviction, ask — told the way she would tell it to someone sitting across from her at a dinner table. Every sentence here can be lifted, shortened, or rearranged into a letter, an email, a speech, or a social post.

---

My mom's name was Sue Chase.

If you asked her friends about her, every single one of them would tell you the same thing — that when Sue looked at you, when Sue listened to you, you felt like you were her only and very best friend in the world. That was her gift. She made people feel seen.

And on Christmas morning 2017, she was dying.

I knew it before anyone said it out loud. Because of my background and my experience, I understood what the treatment options were, and I had watched her try every single one. There was nothing left. The team had done everything they could. No more appointments that mattered. No more medications that would turn the tide. Only a miracle could save her now, and I already knew the miracle wasn't coming.

But my mom never knew that. Or if she did, she never let it stop her. She fought with a kind of grit I will admire for the rest of my life. She had strength I still cannot fathom. She never gave up hope — not once, not until the very end.

Christmas morning is supposed to feel like joy. Like gratitude. Like the celebration of a Savior born into the world. That Christmas morning felt like none of those things. It felt out of place. It felt like the air had been let out of the room.

I sat with her and I had nothing to give her. No gift in the world could bring her joy anymore, because hope — the kind the doctors can offer — was done. And in that silence, in that hollow, unbearable silence, God winked at me.

Words came out of my mouth before I knew I was going to say them.

*Mom — what if we started raising money? What if we tried to find a cure for Parkinson's?*

And my mom lit up.

I watched her heart grow right there in front of me. She looked at me and she said, *Let's do that. Let's make sure no other family has to go through this.*

That was the beginning.

In April of 2018, we started the Chasing a Cure Parkinson's Foundation — a name that blends my family's name, Chase, with my husband Rob's family name, Spalding. Because Parkinson's took from both sides. Because this fight was never just mine. My mom helped shape the vision. She was there at the beginning.

She did not get to see the rest.

Just after the next Christmas, Parkinson's took her from us. On her last conscious night, before hospice began and before she went home to heaven, she made me promise her something. She asked me to never stop. Never stop until a cure is found.

Awareness is important. But awareness is not what families are begging for at 3 a.m. in a hospital room. Awareness is not what I was holding in my hands that Christmas morning when I knew my mother was leaving me. A cure is what families deserve.

So here is what this foundation is. No one here takes a salary — not Rob, not me, not a single board member. We keep operations as lean as a foundation legally can: the costs to run events, process your donation, and keep the lights on. Everything beyond that flows directly to The Michael J. Fox Foundation and Team Fox, which is the most rigorous, most respected Parkinson's research engine in the world. Parkinson's has touched both of our families. My godfather Homer. Rob's aunt Melva. This is not abstract to us, and we don't talk about it like it is.

I don't want your pity. I want your partnership. Ten million people are living with this disease right now. One million of them are in this country. Ninety thousand more will be diagnosed this year — one every six minutes — and not one of them has a cure to go home to.

But the science is moving. That is the part I want you to hear. The science *is moving*, and it is close enough that funding it now, funding it today, funding it this year, actually changes when we get there. That's not a marketing line. That's what my career has taught me.

So I am keeping my promise to Sue Chase. And I am asking you to help me keep it.

Every dollar raised is a dollar closer to the morning when a daughter sits next to her mother on Christmas and does not have to count the time she has left. Every dollar is a promise kept — not just to my mom, but to every mother, every father, every grandparent, every person whose family is living inside this fight right now.

My mom fought with everything she had. Her miracle didn't come.

Let's be somebody else's.

*— Wendy*

Wendy Chase · Co-founder

## 02 · Her Character

### Who Wendy is on the page.

If you're writing as Wendy, or quoting her, or writing about the foundation in a way that needs to *feel* like her, these are the traits to keep in your head. She is a specific person, not a template.

**She is a scientist first, and a daughter second — but never one without the other.**

Wendy does not talk about Parkinson's in abstractions. She knows the biology. She knows the funding pipeline. She knows what a Phase II trial costs and why Team Fox is the right partner. That expertise gives her standing. But she never leads with it — she leads with Sue, with her godfather, with Rob's aunt. The science is the evidence. The family is the reason.

**She is direct. She does not perform grief.**

Wendy will tell you her mother died. She will not dwell on it. She will not ask you to feel bad. She will tell you what she did next, and what she's asking you to do now. The grief is in the room — she doesn't have to decorate it.

**She is warm without being soft.**

She says *family* a lot. She uses first names. She means them. But underneath the warmth there is a spine of conviction that doesn't bend: **the cure is close, we are funding it, your gift flows directly to the work.** She is polite, but she's not here to be charming.

**When she says "partnership," she means something specific.**

A partner gets told what happened to their money. Wendy follows up — not with a generic receipt, but with something real: a research milestone, a trial update from MJFF, a line about what this year's event funded. Partners are on a first-name basis with her. They hear about the concert before the public does. They know where the foundation stands, not just where it's headed. When she uses the word "partner" in copy, it should be backed by that reality — not deployed as a warmer synonym for "donor."

**She is hopeful on purpose.**

Hope, for Wendy, is not a mood. It's a decision. She chooses it because the alternative — despair, cynicism, performance — doesn't fund any science. When she says *the science is moving*, she means it literally, and she expects you to take her seriously.

> "I don't want your pity. I want your partnership."

## 03 · Cadence

### How she talks — rhythm & shape.

Wendy's sentences have a recognizable shape. Match the shape and the voice follows.

**Short sentences carry the weight.**

When something matters, Wendy says it in five words or fewer. *Sue died that December. There is no cure. Not yet. That's the whole thing.* The short sentences are never dramatic — they're just the truth, put plainly.

**Long sentences build the argument.**

When she explains, she uses flowing sentences with clear commas — she'll list three things, she'll stack a claim on top of a qualifier, she'll pull you through the reasoning and then land on something short. *Because the science is moving, and it is close enough that funding it today actually changes when we get there — that's not a marketing line, that's what my career has taught me.*

**She uses the rule of three.**

*Built from love. Backed by science. Focused on the cure.* Three is her number. Watch for it in her writing: three pillars, three promises, three facts, three asks. When you need to make something feel settled, use three.

**She repeats herself on purpose.**

*Fund the science. Fund the hope. Fund the work.* Repetition is how she lands the point. Don't apologize for it. Don't "vary your language." When a phrase is working, say it again.

**She ends where she began.**

Almost every long-form thing she writes closes with a callback to the opening — usually to Sue, or to the promise. That loop is what makes the piece feel complete instead of ended.

## 05 · Guardrails

### What she doesn't say.

Wendy's voice is defined as much by what she refuses as by what she says. If any of these appear in copy under her name, something has gone wrong.

**She would say:**

- **Live with Parkinson's.** A state of being, not a verdict.
- **Family.** Hers, yours, the ones waiting.
- **Fund** the science. **Move** it forward. **Advance** it.
- **The cure.** Not "a cure" — the cure. Specific. Real.
- **Not yet.** Two words that hold every other hopeful thing she says.
- **Breakthrough.** Because that's what the money buys.
- **Sue.** Her mother has a name. Use it.
- **Volunteer-run. No salaries.** True, specific, defensible.
- **Directly to research through MJFF and Team Fox.** Names the pathway.

**She would not say:**

- **Fight.** We're not fighting Parkinson's — we're funding the end of it.
- **Suffer / sufferer / victim.** Reductive and inaccurate.
- **Battle**, **warrior**, **brave journey.** Disease-charity boilerplate.
- **Awareness** as a goal. Awareness is a step toward funding. Funding is the goal.
- **Give back.** Too generic. Say what the money *does.*
- **Help us help them.** She doesn't position donors as saviors.
- **100% of every dollar / every penny / zero overhead.** We do have operating costs (events, processing fees, tools). We never claim otherwise. Say *"directly to research,"* *"no salaries,"* or *"as lean as a foundation legally can."*
- **Exclamation points.** She doesn't need them. Neither do you.

## 06 · In Conversation

### How she'd answer.

Real prompts she gets asked. The answers below are written in her voice and can be lifted as quotes, used in interviews, or adapted for talking points.

---

**Why did you start the foundation?**

> Because on Christmas morning 2017, I sat with my mom and I had nothing to give her. I understood the treatment options — I'd watched her try every single one. There was nothing left. And in the silence of that morning, words came out of my mouth before I knew I was going to say them: *Mom — what if we started raising money? What if we tried to find a cure for Parkinson's?* And she lit up. She said, *Let's do that. Let's make sure no other family has to go through this.* On her last conscious night, she made me promise to never stop. *Chasing a Cure* is that promise.

---

**What makes you different from other Parkinson's charities?**

> Three things. No one here draws a salary — not Rob, not me, not a single board member — so our costs are the bare minimum a foundation can legally run on. We route the funds through The Michael J. Fox Foundation, so the research is world-class. And we're founder-led by two people with over four decades of pharma experience between them. You're not donating into a bureaucracy. You're handing a dollar to someone who knows exactly where it's going.

---

**How close is a cure, really?**

> Closer than most people think, and further than any of us would like. The science *is moving*. There are therapies in trials right now that would have been science fiction when my mother was diagnosed. Whether they land in five years or fifteen depends, in part, on whether we keep funding them. That's the honest answer.

---

**What would you say to someone whose parent was just diagnosed?**

> That's heavy. *[pause]* That's hard. I would encourage them to find the best care team they can — do your homework to find the right support. I would encourage them to ask every question, even the ones they're afraid to ask, because knowing is always better than not knowing. And I would encourage them to stay in it. Not because it's easy. Not because the outcome is guaranteed. But because the science is genuinely moving — there are therapies in trials right now that didn't exist when my mom was diagnosed — and because the people who stay in it, who keep pursuing, who don't let the diagnosis be the last word, give themselves and the people they love the best possible chance. I won't tell you it ends the way you want it to. I can't promise that. What I can tell you is that hope is not naive here. The science earns it.

---

**Why should I give $100 to you instead of a bigger charity?**

> Most foundations carry real overhead — staff, offices, fundraising departments, administrative layers. That's not a criticism; it's just how most of them were built. We looked at that model and made a different decision on purpose. We designed this foundation from day one to operate on the bare minimum — no salaries, no paid staff, costs limited to what it actually takes to run events and process donations — so that as much of every gift as possible flows directly to The Michael J. Fox Foundation and Team Fox. That is not an accident. It is not a phase we're in. It is the architecture of this organization, and it will stay that way. So your $100 doesn't disappear into an institution. It reaches the best Parkinson's research engine in the world with almost nothing taken off the top — and because Rob and I run this ourselves, we can tell you exactly where it went.

## 07 · By Channel

### Her voice, tuned for the room.

Same voice everywhere, different volume. Think of these as dials, not different voices.

**With someone who doesn't know her yet**

Before deploying the full voice, Wendy earns the right to be direct. With a cold contact — someone who hasn't been to the concert, doesn't know Sue's name, hasn't heard her speak — she leads with something the other person already cares about: a shared connection, a relevant number, a question rather than a statement. She doesn't open with the ask or the origin story. She opens with the reason this specific person might care. The full narrative comes second. The ask comes third. Skipping the warm-up is what makes the voice feel presumptuous to people who haven't met her; following it is what makes them feel like she did her homework.

**In a personal email or letter**

She writes like she's talking to one person. *Hi Jennifer.* She opens with something real — a detail about them, or a moment from her week. She says what she's writing about early, usually in the first three sentences. She closes with gratitude and a first name. She does not sign as "Wendy Chase, Co-founder." Just *Wendy.*

**On stage or in a video**

She slows down. She uses Sue's name at least once. She pauses after the short sentences. She ends with one of the three anchor lines — usually *Built from love. Backed by science. Focused on the cure.* — because it's designed to be said out loud.

**On social media**

One idea per post. No thread-bait. No hashtag soup. She might quote one of her own lines, or one number, or one sentence from this document. She does not chase trends. She does not use memes. She does not explain the urgency — she *names* it.

**In a press quote**

She speaks in full sentences that stand alone. She always has one number and one name ready: *Ninety thousand new diagnoses a year. My mother was one of them.* Reporters take that quote every time.

**In a sponsorship conversation**

She's professional, specific, and quick to the point. She doesn't pitch emotion — she pitches alignment. She names the sponsor's audience, the foundation's audience, and the overlap. Then she asks for a specific thing: a check amount, a table, a logo placement, a match. She is comfortable being direct because she is asking for the research, not for herself.

When a sponsor asks how a volunteer-run organization stays accountable, she doesn't deflect. She names the structure: MJFF and Team Fox receive the funds and report on them publicly — that's the accountability layer, and it's more rigorous than most foundations offer. Rob and Wendy provide their pharma backgrounds as the vetting layer. The event is the delivery mechanism. She separates these clearly and lets the sponsor ask follow-up questions rather than pre-answering everything defensively. Defensiveness reads as insecurity. Clarity reads as confidence.

## 09 · The Renewal Moment

### How she sounds when someone already gave.

The renewal ask is the hardest one to get right. The donor already said yes once. They're deciding whether you were worth it. This is a different rhetorical situation than the first ask — she's no longer establishing credibility, she's delivering on the partnership she promised — and the voice shifts accordingly.

**In a thank-you note**

She writes it herself, and it sounds like it. She doesn't thank the donor for "your generous support" — she thanks them for something specific: the amount, the table they bought, the friend they brought. She says what their gift is doing, in one sentence, concretely. She closes with Sue's name or the promise. She does not use a template. A templated thank-you is the fastest way to turn a partner back into a donor.

**In an impact update**

She leads with what happened, not what it means. *The 2024 concert raised $X. That went to MJFF's [specific program].* Then she tells them why that matters — one paragraph, not five. She acknowledges what she doesn't know yet: the science is moving, the timeline is uncertain, the work isn't done. Donors who gave because she was honest the first time expect her to stay honest. False certainty in an update destroys more trust than it builds.

**In a year-two ask**

She doesn't pretend nothing has changed. She acknowledges the year — what the foundation did, what the research moved, what she's asking for now and why now matters. She connects the new ask to what the donor's previous gift did. The structure is: *you did this → it produced this → here's the next step → that's why I'm asking again.* She does not start year-two copy with the origin story — the donor knows it. She starts where they left off.

**The one thing she never does at renewal**

She doesn't guilt. She doesn't imply that a lapsed donor let someone down. If someone didn't give last year and she's reaching out again, she writes as if the relationship is intact — because it might be, and assuming otherwise ends it. She re-earns the ask by leading with what happened since they last talked, not by reminding them of what they didn't do.
